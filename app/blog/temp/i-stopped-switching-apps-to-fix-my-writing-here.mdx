# I Stopped Switching Apps to Fix My iPhone Writing. Here's What Changed.

You're halfway through a message to your manager. You know exactly what you want to say — it just came out wrong. Too casual. A little clunky. So you do the thing you've done a hundred times: copy the text, open ChatGPT, paste it in, wait, copy the result, switch back, paste it again. Fix your writing on iPhone without switching apps? Felt impossible. The whole process took two minutes for one sentence. And then you had to remember where you were.

That was my workflow for longer than I want to admit.

{/* <!-- IMAGE: Screenshot showing the copy-paste loop between Messages and a separate AI app on iPhone --> */}

## The Real Problem Isn't Your Writing

Here's what I kept getting wrong: I thought the issue was my phrasing. That if I could just write better drafts, I wouldn't need to fix them.

But that wasn't it.

The issue was the workflow. The moment something needed polishing — a tone that felt off, a sentence that was too long, a reply that needed to sound more professional — I was immediately dragged out of whatever I was doing. Into a different app. Into a different mental context. And then back again.

That context-switching is a tax. Not just on time, but on momentum. Every time you break flow to fix one sentence, you lose the thread of what you were actually trying to do.

> The friction people feel isn't about grammar. It's about being pulled out of the moment every time they need help.

I started noticing how often I just... didn't fix things. Not because I couldn't, but because the process felt like too much effort for one message. So I'd send something that wasn't quite right, or spend longer than I should second-guessing a reply.

That was the real cost.

## What I Actually Needed

I didn't want AI to write for me. I just wanted something that could fix what I'd already written — without making me stop, switch, wait, and switch back.

That sounds simple. It took me a while to find something that actually did it.

The closest thing most people reach for is Grammarly. And it's fine for catching errors. But it doesn't change tone. It doesn't rephrase the whole sentence when the grammar is technically correct but the message just sounds wrong. It corrects. It doesn't transform.

Apple Intelligence's Writing Tools are genuinely useful — if you have an iPhone 15 Pro or newer, and if your device is set to English (US). Most of the iPhone users I know don't meet both conditions. And even for those who do, the options are limited: Friendly, Professional, Concise. Fixed presets with no way to adjust what "professional" actually means to you.

What I needed was something more flexible. And something that didn't add steps — it needed to remove them.

## What Happened When I Found It

I came across [RewriteMate](https://www.rewritemate.app) while looking for a way to get Apple Intelligence-style writing tools on an older phone. It's an AI keyboard extension for iOS — which means it lives inside the keyboard itself, available in any app where you can type.

The first time I used it, I was writing a reply in Slack. I'd typed something that came across as more blunt than I intended. I selected the text, tapped "Change Tone to Professional," and it was done. In the same app. Without leaving Slack. Without copying anything.

That was the moment I understood what the friction had actually cost me.

It's not that the fix took two minutes before and thirty seconds now. It's that the fix no longer required a decision. There was no calculus about whether it was worth opening another app. The keyboard was already there.

## The Features I Actually Use

I want to be specific here, because "AI writing assistant" covers a lot of ground and most of it isn't what RewriteMate actually does.

The command bar sits above the keyboard whenever text is selected. One tap gets you to the most common actions: Proofread Text, Change Tone to Professional, Change Tone to Casual, Paraphrase, Shorten, Expand, Simplify. These aren't suggestions — they rewrite the selected text in place and give you the result immediately.

The one I use most is Paraphrase. When something is grammatically fine but just sounds off, paraphrasing usually fixes it faster than trying to manually rework the sentence. It keeps the meaning, changes the wording, and most of the time lands closer to what I was trying to say.

> One tap. Same app. No switching. That's it.

There's also a full command library with reply suggestions — Quick Reply, Polite Reply, Agree Reply, Decline Reply — which I use when I'm responding to long messages and don't want to draft from scratch. You highlight the message you received, tap the reply type you need, and it generates a response based on the context.

And for power users, there's a prompt editor where every command is fully visible and editable. The "Change Tone to Casual" prompt, for example, can be customized to specify exactly what casual means to you — whether that's informal but not slangy, or friendly but still precise. That level of control is what separates it from fixed-preset tools.

## It Works Everywhere. That Part Matters.

One thing I didn't expect to matter as much as it does: the keyboard works in every app. Messages, Mail, Instagram, Slack, Notes, Safari, anything with a text field.

That sounds obvious for a keyboard. But most AI writing tools are either a separate app (copy-paste required) or embedded in one specific app. The fact that RewriteMate is system-level — accessible in the same way the emoji keyboard or autocorrect is — means it's always there when you need it and invisible when you don't.

You stop thinking about it as a tool you use. It becomes part of how you type.

## The Thing That Actually Changed

The copy-paste ritual is gone. That's the most straightforward answer.

But the thing that changed more than I expected was how I write now. Because the friction is lower, I actually fix things I used to let slide. A reply that needed one more pass. A caption that wasn't quite right. A work message where the tone was slightly off.

Before, those things got sent as-is because fixing them cost too much attention. Now they get a one-tap polish in seconds, and I move on.

That's the version of AI writing assistance I actually wanted. Not a tool that writes for me. One that makes fixing my own writing cost almost nothing.
